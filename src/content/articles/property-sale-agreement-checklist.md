A property sale agreement is not just paperwork. It is the legal map of the deal. If the map is weak, even a promising transaction can end in delay, dispute, or loss.

Before signing, confirm that the seller is the registered owner and that the title number on the agreement matches the records at the land registry. A mismatch in names, title references, or plot description is a major red flag that should be resolved first.

Review encumbrances carefully. Charges, cautions, restrictions, easements, and court orders can affect transfer and use of the property. The agreement should state how any existing encumbrance will be discharged and by what date.

Check the purchase price structure and payment terms. The agreement should clearly define the deposit amount, where it is held, the balance timeline, and what happens if completion is delayed. Vague payment clauses usually create conflict later.

Completion documents must be listed in detail. This includes the original title, transfer instruments, land rates clearance, land rent clearance (where applicable), consent to transfer where required, and identity documents for verification. If the document list is incomplete, completion can stall.

The completion date should be realistic and tied to conditions that can actually be met. Add a clear notice mechanism for extensions and a transparent default clause that explains remedies for both buyer and seller.

Include practical possession terms. State when vacant possession is given, what fixtures are included, and whether there are existing tenants. If there is tenancy, attach tenancy details and responsibility for rent apportionment.

Tax and statutory costs should be allocated clearly. Confirm who handles stamp duty, registration fees, legal fees, valuation fees, and any agency commission. Unclear cost clauses often become last-minute arguments that delay transfer.

Finally, ensure the dispute resolution clause is balanced and enforceable. A strong agreement protects both sides and reduces the chance of expensive litigation. If you do not fully understand a clause, pause and get legal clarification before signing.

Good agreements do not only close deals. They preserve trust, reduce risk, and make completion predictable.
